What is Calcium?

Calcium was first isolated by the British chemist Sir Humphry Davy in 1808. Calcium with atomic number 20 is represented in the periodic table with the symbol Ca. Calcium (Ca) is a vital element that aids our bones stay tough and capable of supporting our load.

The Importance of Calcium

  • It is vital for bones and teeth.
  • It assists in muscle movement by carrying messages from the brain to all body parts.
  • It is essential for healthy nerves and muscle tissue.
  • It helps our muscles, heart, and nerves work properly.
  • It is critical for the development, health, and continued maintenance of bone.
  • It plays a role in blood clotting.
  • It helps maintain the action of heart muscle by relaxing the smooth muscles around blood vessels.
  • It can help balance muscle contraction.

Where can we find Calcium?

Calcium can be found in a variety of foods and supplements. Here are some common sources:
Dietary Sources:

  1. Dairy Products: Milk, cheese, and yogurt are rich in calcium.
  2. Leafy Green Vegetables: Kale, collard greens, broccoli, and bok choy are good vegetable sources.
  3. Fish: Sardines and salmon, especially with bones, provide a good amount of calcium.
  4. Fortified Foods: Some cereals, juices, and plant-based milk (like almond, soy, or rice milk) are fortified with calcium.
  5. Nuts and Seeds: Almonds and sesame seeds contain calcium.
  6. Legumes: Beans, lentils, and chickpeas can contribute to calcium intake.
  7. Tofu: Especially when prepared with calcium sulfate, tofu can be a good source.
Supplements:
Calcium supplements are available in various forms, such as calcium carbonate and calcium citrate. These can be found in pharmacies, health food stores, and online.

Other Sources:
  • Calcium-Fortified Water: Some bottled water brands add calcium.
  • Calcium-Rich Snacks: Some snacks, like certain granola bars and crackers, are fortified with calcium.

    • How do we use Calcium?

    1. Take calcium citrate with or without food. Take calcium carbonate with food. The acid the stomach makes while eating helps the body absorb calcium carbonate1.
    2. It is best to take calcium in smaller doses. Smaller doses most often means 500 milligrams or less at a time.
    3. You can consume calcium from foods or supplements, or by using calcium that already exists in the body by taking it from bones. The calcium that is "borrowed" from the bones should ideally be replaced by eating high-calcium foods.
    4. People commonly take calcium by mouth for the treatment and prevention of low calcium levels, muscle cramps, osteoporosis, softening of the bones, and PMS. It is also used for high blood pressure, cancer, stroke, and many other conditions, but there is no good scientific evidence to support many of these other uses.
    Tips for Optimal Use:
    • Balance with Other Nutrients: Ensure a balanced intake of other nutrients like magnesium and phosphorus, which are important for bone health.
    • Avoid Excessive Intake: Excessive calcium intake can lead to health issues, including kidney stones and cardiovascular problems. Stick to recommended daily amounts unless otherwise advised by a healthcare professional.
    Recommended Daily Intake:
    The recommended daily intake of calcium varies by age, gender, and life stage. For example:
    • Adults (19-50 years): Around 1,000 mg/day
    • Women over 50 and men over 70: 1,200 mg/day
    • Teens (9-18 years): 1,300 mg/d
